Chopin Gallery

Explore portraits, manuscripts, historical locations, and other visual artifacts related to Chopin's life and work.

Portrait by Eugène Delacroix

1838

Eugène Delacroix, a close friend of Chopin, created this portrait showing the composer alongside George Sand. The painting captures Chopin at the piano with Sand listening nearby, illustrating their personal and artistic relationship.

This unfinished portrait is housed in the Louvre Museum in Paris.

Portrait by Ary Scheffer

1847

This portrait by Dutch-French Romantic painter Ary Scheffer shows Chopin two years before his death. It captures the composer's frail condition, with his characteristic elegant poise nonetheless preserved.

The portrait is now in the collection of the Dordrechts Museum in the Netherlands.

Manuscript: Nocturne in E♭ major

Op. 9 No. 2, 1832

This manuscript of Chopin's most famous nocturne reveals his meticulous notation and attention to detail. The manuscript contains performance directions and fingerings in the composer's hand.

Chopin was known for his precise notation, especially regarding pedal markings and expressive indications.

Manuscript: Ballade No. 1 in G minor

Op. 23, 1835

This manuscript shows Chopin's revolutionary Ballade form, one of his most significant contributions to piano literature. The Ballade No. 1 was dedicated to Baron Nathaniel von Stockhausen.

The manuscript shows several revisions and changes, offering insight into Chopin's compositional process.

Żelazowa Wola

Chopin's Birthplace

Chopin was born in this manor house in Żelazowa Wola, Poland, where his father worked as a tutor. Today, the house has been transformed into a museum dedicated to the composer.

The surrounding park features sculptures and hosts regular concerts of Chopin's music during the summer months.

George Sand's Estate at Nohant

France

Chopin spent seven summers at the country estate of his companion, writer George Sand, in Nohant, France. These summers were among his most productive periods, during which he composed many significant works.

The peaceful environment and Sand's support provided Chopin with ideal conditions for composition.

Pleyel Piano

Chopin's Preferred Instrument

Chopin preferred Pleyel pianos above all others, appreciating their sensitive action and tonal qualities. He once remarked, "When I feel out of sorts, I play on an Érard piano where I easily find a ready-made tone. But when I feel in good form and strong enough to find my own tone, I need a Pleyel piano."

This particular Pleyel piano was used by Chopin during his later Paris years.

Death Mask

1849

After Chopin's death on October 17, 1849, a cast was made of his face—a common practice for notable figures of the time. The death mask preserves the composer's delicate features and has become an important historical artifact.

A cast of Chopin's hands was also made, preserving the shape of the hands that created some of the most celebrated piano music ever written.
